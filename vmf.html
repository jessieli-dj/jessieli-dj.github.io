The Project

This was a 13-week project to design an alternative to the current Vancouver Mural Festival mural walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community.

My team created an art kit for BC grade 6 and 7 classroom teachers, including: an instructions booklet, art lesson planning cards, and mural passports for students. Every year, the kit would feature a different artist from the annual Festival, to help spread awareness about mural art in the community.

My Role

I conducted an interview with one of our stakeholders as part of user research, as well as documented ethnographies. I also worked on creating user journey frameworks and personas as part of the design process. In the prototyping stage, I worked primarily on the instructions booklet and mural passports, and assisted with content strategy for the lesson cards.

Initial Research

My team went on a private walking tour of the murals to observe the process. I documented the tour through photos, especially in areas where I observed potential pain points.

Insight: Accessibility issues, including physical mobility and hearing difficulties.

We reached out to key stakeholders from the organization, including VMF executives, curators, and local businesses. I conducted an interview, and took notes for others, which were later synthesized into major themes.

Insight: Acquiring walls for new murals can be time-consuming because not everyone is familiar with VMF’s process or with mural art.

Journey Mapping

From our research, we identified two opportunities: mural wall acquisition and private walking tours. We created journey frameworks for both of our opportunities in order to identify specific pain points.

I was primarily responsible for creating the journey framework for the wall acquisition process and helped flesh out touchpoints for the walking tour journey framework.

After consultation with our client, we chose to address the private walking tours because that was the area they had the least amount of resources to work on until now.

Walking Tour Pain Points

  • The tour is physically and mentally taxing, as the tour guide is providing information about murals as the group walked through alleyways and sidewalks.
  • Because murals were located in alleyways, sidewalks, and across busy streets, accessibility was a problem for individuals with mobility and hearing concerns.
  • The ending of the tour was abrupt and lacked extension to the annual festival.

Personas

We created three personas: the VMF tour guide, an art advocate, and a local art enthusiast. I focused primarily on developing the persona of the VMF tour guide, specifically the pains, behaviours, needs and goals. This helped guide us in creating content for our prototypes, by understanding the tour guide's (and by extension, the organization's) intentions in the stories they tell.

Framing

Because we could not significantly change the format of the tours due to lack of staff and the fixed locations of the murals, we decided to create an an alternative to the current walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community.

Ideating + Sketching

My team ran a 3-day condensed design sprint to rapidly ideate and prototype, using our framing question and detailed user journey framework to guide us in our ideating.

The outcome of our sprint were three fleshed out mobile app concept proposals:

  • Mural Hunt - A mobile app that asks players to visit murals to receive prizes for redemption at the annual festival.
  • Mural Bot - An AI chatbot leveraging Facebook Messenger to talk to users as they go on a self-guided tour.
  • Coming Soon - A series of physical art installations to give the local community a preview of murals that are coming soon.

I was primarily responsible for Mural Hunt. I proposed combining gaming with viewing the murals, to engage younger audiences with mural art in a fun way. The idea was a mobile app that would send an alert and a time limited mission to complete if the player was near a mural. The player would earn prizes that could be redeemed at the annual mural festival. This would increase active engagement with the murals and provide incentive to attend the festival.

Some concerns were discoverability and if someone from outside both Mount Pleasant and the mural art community would go there just to play the game. In addition, players with limited mobility may struggle with completing the task within the time limit.

I worked with one teammate to refine the idea and create the user interface. To address discoverability, we specified our target audience to be people who were already in Mount Pleasant. Checkpoints would be placed throughout the area to invite people nearby to download the app to begin playing. Players would receive a list of murals at each checkpoint and earn stamps by doing activities at each mural. We also removed the time limit to allow players to move at their own pace.

Co-Creation Workshop

Planning

We presented our concept proposals to our client during a co-creation workshop for their feedback. I worked with one teammate to plan 4 activities for the workshop to help generate more ideas and specific designs directly with our client. We took inspiration from IDEO's design kit and JISC's Planning a Participatory Workshop. We adapted each activity to fit our smaller group of participants, as well as to fit within our time constraint of 2 hours for the workshop.

I was primarily responsible for the 2 ideating activities. For the first activity, I ensured that it was not too narrowed in scope, as we wanted to give the client freedom to come up with ideas beyond those we had presented already. Because the second ideating activity was to lead into a design/visualization activity, I designed it to be more structured and targeted than the first.

Outcome

These activities helped us uncover more of our client's long-term goals and values, including promoting mural literacy across Vancouver. Mural literacy is the ability to engage with, interpret and understand the differences between murals, street art, graffiti and vandalism.

We found an opportunity to do this through outreach into BC arts education in the form of mural-themed kits for classroom teachers and students. This would fulfil Vancouver Mural Festival's goals of improving mural literacy and help foster positive relationships within the local community.

User Testing

We conducted user tests with 3 teacher candidates from the University of British Columbia (UBC), and 1 classroom teacher with our initial prototypes. I helped come up with user testing questions, as well as conducted an interview with a teacher candidate while taking detailed notes.

We organized the user testing feedback into two levels of priority based on time constraints: changes that we could and should make, and changes that would be nice to have but not critical.

This allowed us to manage our time efficiently before our deadline, in order to deliver a high quality product proposal on time.

Instructions Booklet

I worked on the information and visual design, content strategy and copywriting.

The information booklet contains a kit overview with value proposition for teachers, detailed instructions on the supplies inside the kit, as well as an introduction to the featured artist of the kit. Each page of the booklet includes an image of each item in the kit and a brief description of how they are meant to be used.

The copy in the booklet was written with a friendly, conversational tone in order to make the kit feel more welcoming for teachers of all ages and comfort levels with art lessons.

Iterating

I began with whiteboard wireframes to block out the layout of the information and how the instructions would be presented. Since it was for an art kit, I wanted to be more creative with it so I planned to have an accordian fold. However, I realized this would be impractical for quick referencing, so I scrapped that idea.

During our user testing with teacher candidates, they reinforced that the copywriting should be more descriptive and conversational. In the final design, I changed the copy to describe what teachers could do with the items rather than what they’re intended to do, to avoid creating limitations for teachers.

In the first iteration, each spread had a light-hearted one-liner to give a friendly, conversational tone. However, though it was well-received, this was replaced with more detailed descriptions of the kit items, as well as a kit overview with value proposition for teachers. These were changes made after user testing because teachers wanted to know up front what benefits the kit could offer.

Mural Passport

I worked on the visual design and content strategy.

Each page in the passport includes information about the mural and artist, as well as a photo to be inclusive of students who may not be able to visit the murals in person. Included in the kit are mural sticker sheets, which teachers may use as prizes for visiting the murals. This gamification aspect brings an element of joy to the experience and provides incentive to engage more meaningfully with the murals.

The cover of the passport showcases artwork done by the featured artist of each year's Festival, to create a tangible connection from the classroom to the annual Festival in August.

Iterating

During user testing, there was a suggestion to add reflection questions as an alternative to visiting the murals physically, as some students may not have the resources to do so. This would also encourage students to practice their critical thinking, which was a major goal of the BC curriculum. In the final design, we created reflection questions for each mural that would provoke reflection from students about the themes of the murals.

We also received feedback the language used might be unfamiliar to students, especially ELL students. Rather than simplifying the language and losing the nuances of certain terms, we added a section at the back with definitions and pronunciations. This provides students with a way to actively expand their vocabulary.

Lesson Planning Cards

I worked primarily on content strategy and also helped with visual design and copywriting.

Lesson activities were created after extensive research into the BC curriculum for Art for grades 6 and 7. The activities focused on encouraging students to develop a deeper interest in art, and build on skills such as critical thinking, teamwork, and self-reflection.

Each card has a border at the top, colour-coded to a “Big Idea”, taken straight from the BC curriculum. We did this in order to relate the ideas behind the lessons to teachers in terms they would already be familiar with. There are symbols for duration, so teachers can easily sort through the cards and scan for relevant information quickly.

Iterating

I worked with one teammate to block out the information design of the cards using whiteboard wireframes. We considered various sets of icons and shapes to represent the difficulty level and duration of the activities. However, we received feedback from user testing that a difficulty rating system would be impractical, as different students and classes have different skill levels. As a result, we removed the difficulty ratings.

Reflection

Because this project was a collaboration with a client, I was required to consider feasibility and cost more than in other hypothetical school projects. When selecting what the kit would contain, I looked up production costs in order to judge whether the benefits of including the item outweighed the potential cost for our client.

I also gained more knowledge and familiarity with designing in the education domain, as our target audience were teachers and students of grades 6 and 7 classrooms. I conducted research into the current BC curriculum requirements, as well as arts activities that helped students build fundamental skills such as social bonding, critical thinking, and learning through play.
