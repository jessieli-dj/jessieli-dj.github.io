Vancouver Mural Festival Classroom Kit

This was a 13-week project to design an alternative to the current Vancouver Mural Festival mural walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community. The outcome was a kit with art lesson materials for grades 6 and 7 classrooms.

My role included:

  • Rapidly prototyping visual designs and interactions
  • Applying user-centered design methods such as personas and user journey frameworks
  • Planning and facilitating user testing
  • Planning a co-creation workshop

Tools

  • Illustrator
  • InDesign
  • Photoshop

When

May - August 2018

Journey Mapping

After speaking with stakeholders and doing first-hand research into our client's services, we identified two opportunities: mural wall acquisition and private walking tours. We created journey frameworks for both of our opportunities in order to identify specific pain points.

The walking tour was an under-developed area for our client due to lack of resources, and information was more accessible to us so we moved forward with opportunities in this area.

Pain Points

  • The tour is physically and mentally taxing, as the tour guide is providing information about murals as the group walked through alleyways and sidewalks.
  • Because murals were located in alleyways, sidewalks, and across busy streets, accessibility was a problem for individuals with mobility and hearing concerns.
  • The ending of the tour was abrupt and lacked extension to the annual festival.

Personas

By understanding the organization's intentions in the stories they tell through a stakeholder persona, we were able to craft targeted content for our prototypes.

Framing

Due to lack of staff and the fixed locations of the murals, it was not feasible for us to change the format of the walking tours. Therefore, we decided to create an an alternative to the current walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community.

Initial Ideas

Running a 3-day condensed design sprint allowed my team to rapidly ideate and prototype, using our framing question and detailed user journey framework to guide us.

I proposed a gamified mural viewing experience to engage younger audiences in a fun way, working with one teammate to refine the design.

Would people go to the community just to play the game?

Scoping down our target audience to be people already in the neighbourhood allowed us to create more targeted touchpoints, such as physical checkpoints placed throughout the neighbourhood. Players are provided with a list of murals associated with each checkpoint and earn stamps by doing activities at each mural.

Would players with limited mobility feel frustrated by the time limit?

Removing the time limit allows players to move at their own pace, to be more inclusive of players with different mobility needs.

Co-Creation Workshop

Our next step was presenting our concept proposals to our client for their feedback. I worked with one teammate to plan 4 activities for a co-creation workshop to help generate more ideas and specific designs directly with our client.

These activities uncovered more of our client's long-term goals and values, such as promoting mural literacy across Vancouver and creating stronger positive ties to the community.

We identified an opportunity to do this through outreach into BC arts education in the form of mural-themed kits for classroom teachers and students. This would fulfil Vancouver Mural Festival's goals of improving mural literacy and help foster positive relationships within the local community.

User Testing

Conducting user tests with teachers and teacher candidates allowed us to gauge the feasibility and usefulness of our idea with our actual users. I defined user testing questions based on research goals, and facilitated a usability session.

Afterwards, we used affinity diagramming to organize the feedback into two levels of priority: changes that we could and should make, and changes that would be nice to have but not critical. This allowed us to manage our time efficiently to deliver a high quality product proposal by our deadline.

Instructions Booklet

Initially, each spread had a light-hearted one-liner for a friendly, conversational tone. Though well-received, teachers were more interested in what benefits the kit could offer. As a result, I replaced the jokes with descriptions of the kit items, as well as value propositions for teachers.

Mural Passport

Teachers appreciated the inclusion of photos for students who may not have the resources to visit the murals in person, and suggested including reflection questions as an alternative way to earn a prize stamp. This would also encourage students to practice their critical thinking, which was a major goal of the BC curriculum.

We received feedback from teachers that the language used might be unfamiliar to students, especially English language learners. Rather than simplifying the language and losing the nuances of certain terms, we added a section with definitions and pronunciations. This provides students with a way to actively expand their vocabulary.

Lesson Planning Cards

I worked with one teammate to block out the information design of the cards using whiteboard wireframes. We considered various sets of icons and shapes to represent the difficulty level and duration of the activities. However, we received feedback from teachers that different students and classes have different skill levels.

Each card has a border at the top, colour-coded to a “Big Idea”, taken straight from the BC curriculum in order to relate the ideas behind the lessons to teachers in terms they would already be familiar with.

Reflection

Because this project was a collaboration with a client, I was required to consider feasibility and cost more than in other hypothetical school projects. When selecting what the kit would contain, I looked up production costs in order to judge whether the benefits of including the item outweighed the potential cost for our client.

I also gained more knowledge and familiarity with designing in the education domain, as our target audience were teachers and students of grades 6 and 7 classrooms. I conducted research into the current BC curriculum requirements, as well as arts activities that helped students build fundamental skills such as social bonding, critical thinking, and learning through play.
