VMF Classroom Kit

Overview

13-week project to design an alternative to the current Vancouver Mural Festival mural walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community.

I worked with a team of 5 to create an art lesson planning kit for BC grade 6 and 7 classroom teachers, including: an instructions booklet, art lesson planning cards, and mural passports for students.

Skills

  • Interaction design
  • visual design
  • journey mapping
  • user testing

Tools

  • Illustrator
  • InDesign
  • Photoshop

When

May - August 2018

Initial Research

My team went on a private walking tour of the murals to gain a first-hand experience. I identified and documented potential pain points through photos.

Insights: Accessibility issues, including physical mobility and hearing difficulties.

We reached out to key stakeholders from the organization, including VMF executives, curators, and local businesses. I conducted an interview, and took notes for others, which were later synthesized into major themes.

Insights: Acquiring walls for new murals can be time-consuming because not everyone is familiar with VMF’s process or with mural art.

Journey Mapping

From our research, we identified two opportunities: mural wall acquisition and private walking tours. We created journey frameworks for both of our opportunities in order to identify specific pain points.

I was primarily responsible for creating the journey framework for the wall acquisition process and helped flesh out touchpoints for the walking tour journey framework.

Walking Tour Pain Points

  • The tour is physically and mentally taxing, as the tour guide is providing information about murals as the group walked through alleyways and sidewalks.
  • Because murals were located in alleyways, sidewalks, and across busy streets, accessibility was a problem for individuals with mobility and hearing concerns.
  • The ending of the tour was abrupt and lacked extension to the annual festival.

Personas

We created three personas: the VMF tour guide, an art advocate, and a local art enthusiast. I focused primarily on developing the persona of the VMF tour guide, specifically the pains, behaviours, needs and goals. This helped guide us in creating content for our prototypes, by understanding the tour guide's (and by extension, the organization's) intentions in the stories they tell.

Framing

Because we could not significantly change the format of the tours due to lack of staff and the fixed locations of the murals, we decided to create an an alternative to the current walking tour that would increase accessibility and inclusivity of people not already in the mural art community.

Ideating + Sketching

Running a 3-day condensed design sprint allowed my team to rapidly ideate and prototype, using our framing question and detailed user journey framework to guide us.

The outcome of our sprint were three fleshed out mobile app concept proposals. I was primarily responsible for a gamified mural viewing experience called Mural Hunt.

Mural Hunt gamified the mural viewing experience to engage younger audiences in a fun way, by sending players missions to complete at nearby murals. Prizes for completing missions could be redeemed at the annual mural festival, increasing active engagement with the murals and providing incentive to attend the festival.

I worked with one teammate to refine the idea and create the UI.

Consideration 1: Would people go to the community just to play the game?

We scoped down our target audience to be people already in the neighbourhood. Physical checkpoints placed throughout the neighbourhood invited people to download the app. Players are provided with a list of murals associated with each checkpoint and earn stamps by doing activities at each mural.

Consideration 2: Would players with limited mobility feel constrained or frustrated by the time limit?

We removed the time limit to allow players to move at their own pace.

Co-Creation Workshop

Planning

Our next step was presenting our concept proposals to our client for their feedback. I worked with one teammate to plan 4 activities for a co-creation workshop to help generate more ideas and specific designs directly with our client.

These activities uncovered more of our client's long-term goals and values, including promoting mural literacy across Vancouver.

We identified an opportunity to do this through outreach into BC arts education in the form of mural-themed kits for classroom teachers and students. This would fulfil Vancouver Mural Festival's goals of improving mural literacy and help foster positive relationships within the local community.

User Testing

We conducted user tests with three teacher candidates from the University of British Columbia, and one classroom teacher with our initial prototypes. I defined user testing questions based on what we wanted to validate, and facilitated a usability session with a teacher candidate.

We used affinity diagramming to organize the feedback into two levels of priority: changes that we could and should make, and changes that would be nice to have but not critical. This allowed us to manage our time efficiently to deliver a high quality product proposal by our deadline.

Instructions Booklet

During user testing, teacher candidates reinforced that the copy should be more descriptive and conversational. I revised the copy to describe what teachers could do with the items rather than what they’re intended to do, to avoid telling teachers how to do their jobs.

Initially, each spread had a light-hearted one-liner for a friendly, conversational tone. Though well-received, teachers were more interested in what benefits the kit could offer. As a result, I replaced the jokes with descriptions of the kit items, as well as value propositions for teachers.

Mural Passport

Each page includes information about the mural and artist, as well as a photo to be inclusive of students who may not be able to visit the murals in person. Included in the kit are mural sticker sheets, which teachers may use as prizes for visiting the murals. This gamification aspect brings an element of joy to the experience and provides incentive to engage more meaningfully with the murals.

During user testing, teachers appreciated the inclusion of photos for students who may not have the resources to visit the murals in person, and suggested including reflection questions as an alternative way to earn a prize stamp. This would also encourage students to practice their critical thinking, which was a major goal of the BC curriculum.

We also received feedback the language used might be unfamiliar to students, especially English language learners. Rather than simplifying the language and losing the nuances of certain terms, we added a section with definitions and pronunciations. This provides students with a way to actively expand their vocabulary.

Lesson Planning Cards

Lesson activities were created after extensive research into the BC curriculum for Art for grades 6 and 7. The activities focused on encouraging students to develop a deeper interest in art, and build on skills such as critical thinking, teamwork, and self-reflection.

I worked with one teammate to block out the information design of the cards using whiteboard wireframes. We considered various sets of icons and shapes to represent the difficulty level and duration of the activities. However, we received feedback from teachers that different students and classes have different skill levels.

Each card has a border at the top, colour-coded to a “Big Idea”, taken straight from the BC curriculum. We did this in order to relate the ideas behind the lessons to teachers in terms they would already be familiar with. There are symbols for duration, so teachers can easily sort through the cards and scan for relevant information quickly.

Reflection

Because this project was a collaboration with a client, I was required to consider feasibility and cost more than in other hypothetical school projects. When selecting what the kit would contain, I looked up production costs in order to judge whether the benefits of including the item outweighed the potential cost for our client.

I also gained more knowledge and familiarity with designing in the education domain, as our target audience were teachers and students of grades 6 and 7 classrooms. I conducted research into the current BC curriculum requirements, as well as arts activities that helped students build fundamental skills such as social bonding, critical thinking, and learning through play.
